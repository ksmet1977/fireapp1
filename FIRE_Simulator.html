ETF returns
7% mean nominal return, 16% std deviation
The long-run MSCI World / IWDA nominal average (1970–2024) is ~10–11%, but this includes an exceptional post-2008 bull run driven by zero interest rates. Forward-looking estimates are more conservative for several reasons: rate normalisation, geopolitical fragmentation (US-China rivalry, BRICS realignment), deglobalisation pressures, demographic headwinds in Western markets, and energy transition costs. Ray Dalio's framework (Big Debt Cycle, Changing World Order) suggests 4–5% real returns over the next decade for a diversified global portfolio as the US-led global order transitions. Mainstream academic estimates for global equities: 6–8% nominal. The 7% default sits between the optimistic historical average and the Dalio bear case — conservative enough to account for the current geopolitical environment without assuming a full crisis (which is already covered by the Stagflation + 1929 scenario). The 1970s lost decade returned ~4% nominal; the 2000–2010 lost decade ~0%; post-2008 bull run ~14%. Sources: MSCI World index data 1970–2024; Dalio (2021) "The Changing World Order"; Vanguard Capital Markets Model 2024; Dimson, Marsh & Staunton "Global Investment Returns Yearbook".
Cash return
0% nominal — loses real value at inflation rate
Belgian savings accounts have historically yielded 0.11–1.75% nominal in the post-2015 low-rate environment. Modelled as 0% to be conservative and reflect that cash held outside high-yield savings earns effectively nothing in real terms. At 2.5% inflation, €665k in cash loses ~€140k of real value over 10 years. No bonds are modelled — this reflects the actual portfolio composition.
Inflation
3% mean, 1% std deviation, floor −1%
Belgian CPI averaged ~2.2% from 2000–2019 and spiked to 10%+ in 2022. The 3% default reflects a more cautious forward assumption than the ECB's 2% target: structural inflation drivers including energy transition costs, deglobalisation (tariffs raising import prices), ageing population pressures on healthcare and wages, and persistent Belgian wage indexation all suggest inflation is likely to run above the pre-2020 average. The 1% standard deviation reflects normal year-to-year variation. The −1% floor prevents deflationary spirals outside the depression scenario. Source: Statbel Belgian CPI data, ECB inflation targets, NBB economic outlook.
Land / real estate
2.5% nominal appreciation base rate (range 0–10% adjustable)
Belgian bouwgrond (building land) in East Flanders has appreciated at 2–5% nominal per year over 2010–2023, with significant variation depending on location, plot size, and zoning. The slider allows 0% (no appreciation, fully conservative) up to 10%. The 2.5% default reflects a cautious mid-point of the 2–5% observed range, accounting for the possibility that rural land near smaller towns may appreciate more slowly than peri-urban plots, and that the strong 2015–2022 appreciation may not repeat at the same pace. If your land has planning permission or development potential, 4–5% would be more appropriate. Source: Statbel vastgoedstatistieken, Notarisbarometer 2023; CIB Belgium vastgoedbarometer.
+40% of CPI as additional appreciation during stagflation
Real estate prices historically show partial pass-through of inflation — property is a real asset and tends to rise with general price levels, but with a lag and only partially. The 40% figure means that at 8% CPI, land gains an extra ~3.2% on top of the base rate. This is based on academic research into Belgian and European real estate inflation beta, which typically ranges from 30–60% for residential land over 5-year horizons. Sources: Geltner et al. "Real Estate Finance and Investments" (2014); De Wit & Van Dijk (2003) "The Global Determinants of Real Estate Returns."
Belgian capital gains tax on land
Land held >8 years is sold completely tax-free in Belgium (no meerwaardebelasting). Notary and transaction costs of ~1–2% are implicitly absorbed in the conservative appreciation assumption. Source: FOD Financiën, Wetboek van de Inkomstenbelastingen art. 90, 8°.
Pension
Belgian state pension, inflation-indexed from age 67
Belgian wettelijk pensioen is indexed to the consumer price index via the health index mechanism (spilindex). The simulation inflation-indexes the pension amount each year, which reflects actual Belgian pension law. The statutory retirement age is 67 from 2030 onward. Source: Federale Pensioendienst, Wet van 10 augustus 2015.
Stagflation + 1929 scenario
Depression-style crash + 1970s stagflation overlay
The 1929 equity path is derived from the Dow Jones Industrial Average decline of 1929–1932 (−89% peak to trough), scaled to the MSCI World context. The stagflation inflation overlay is based on US CPI during 1973–1982 (peak 14.8% in 1980) applied at a European scale. The combination represents the worst plausible scenario: equities collapse while cost of living rises, destroying both the ETF portfolio and the real value of cash simultaneously. The default path is calibrated to be severe but historically grounded — not a tail risk invented for the app. Sources: Shiller (2005) "Irrational Exuberance"; FRED US CPI data 1970–1985; MSCI World reconstructed pre-1987 data.
Failure definition
Liquid portfolio reaches zero AND land already sold
A simulation path is counted as "failed" only when both the liquid portfolio (ETF + cash) hits zero and the land has already been sold and spent. This means the land sale acts as a mandatory last-resort rescue — failure only occurs if even the land proceeds are exhausted. This is a conservative and realistic definition: it assumes land can always be sold, which may not hold in severe economic conditions. Home equity (50%) is excluded entirely as an additional unlisted buffer.
Monte Carlo method
Shared paths, normally-distributed shocks
Each simulation run generates N random market paths first (using Box-Muller normal distribution), then runs all allocations on the identical paths. This ensures fair comparison — the only difference between 30% and 50% ETF is the allocation, not luck of the draw. Returns and inflation are drawn independently each year (no autocorrelation modelled), which is a simplification — real markets show some momentum and mean-reversion. This makes the model slightly more volatile than reality in short windows but unbiased over long horizons.
