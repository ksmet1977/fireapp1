ETF returns
6% mean nominal return, 16% std deviation
The long-run MSCI World / IWDA nominal average (1970–2024) is ~10–11%, but this includes an exceptional post-2008 bull run driven by zero interest rates. The baseline is set at 6% (reduced from the historical average) to reflect the current structural headwinds: (1) the US fiscal trap — $39T debt means the Fed has limited room to cut rates in a downturn, capping the P/E expansion that drove post-2008 returns; (2) AI bubble risk — the top 5 tech names are 15–18% of the index at 2000-era valuations, creating meaningful concentration risk; (3) financial repression — the likely resolution path for US debt involves holding real yields negative, which historically produces mediocre nominal equity returns; (4) deglobalisation drag — the disinflationary tailwind from cheap global manufacturing is reversing. Sources: MSCI World index data 1970–2024; Dalio (2021) "The Changing World Order"; Vanguard Capital Markets Model 2024; Dimson, Marsh & Staunton "Global Investment Returns Yearbook"; CBO Budget Outlook 2026.
Cash return
0% nominal — loses real value at inflation rate
Belgian savings accounts have historically yielded 0.11–1.75% nominal in the post-2015 low-rate environment. Modelled as 0% to be conservative and reflect that cash held outside high-yield savings earns effectively nothing in real terms. At 3.5% inflation (the baseline assumption), €665k in cash loses ~€200k of real value over 10 years. If you hold cash in a savings account earning 1–2%, adjust the cash return slider accordingly — but note that savings account rates lag inflation and are not guaranteed. Sources: NBB savings rate statistics, Belgian banking federation.
Inflation
3.5% mean, 1% std deviation, floor −1%
Belgian CPI averaged ~2.2% from 2000–2019 and spiked to 10%+ in 2022. The 3.5% default is set above the ECB's 2% target for the following reasons:

Structural drivers keeping inflation elevated: (1) Belgian automatic wage indexation means CPI mechanically feeds into wages and then back into services prices — Belgium has one of the strongest wage-price spiral mechanisms in Europe; (2) energy transition costs (grid upgrades, green levies) are being passed to consumers via energy bills; (3) deglobalisation and tariffs are reversing 30 years of cheap import deflation; (4) ageing population increases healthcare and care costs faster than general CPI.

The US fiscal repression channel: If the US resolves its $39T debt problem through financial repression (holding real yields negative by letting inflation run 3–5%), dollar weakness exports inflation globally. Belgium imports in dollars for energy and many commodities — dollar debasement is partially Belgian inflation.

The 1% standard deviation reflects normal year-to-year variation around the mean. The −1% floor prevents deflationary spirals outside the dedicated depression scenario. Sources: Statbel Belgian CPI data, NBB wage indexation data, ECB inflation targets, IMF World Economic Outlook 2025.
Land / real estate
2.5% nominal appreciation base rate (range 0–10% adjustable)
Belgian bouwgrond (building land) in East Flanders has appreciated at 2–5% nominal per year over 2010–2023, with significant variation depending on location, plot size, and zoning. The slider allows 0% (no appreciation, fully conservative) up to 10%. The 2.5% default reflects a cautious mid-point of the 2–5% observed range, accounting for the possibility that rural land near smaller towns may appreciate more slowly than peri-urban plots, and that the strong 2015–2022 appreciation may not repeat at the same pace. If your land has planning permission or development potential, 4–5% would be more appropriate. Source: Statbel vastgoedstatistieken, Notarisbarometer 2023; CIB Belgium vastgoedbarometer.
+40% of CPI as additional appreciation during stagflation
Real estate prices historically show partial pass-through of inflation — property is a real asset and tends to rise with general price levels, but with a lag and only partially. The 40% figure means that at 8% CPI, land gains an extra ~3.2% on top of the base rate. This is based on academic research into Belgian and European real estate inflation beta, which typically ranges from 30–60% for residential land over 5-year horizons. Sources: Geltner et al. "Real Estate Finance and Investments" (2014); De Wit & Van Dijk (2003) "The Global Determinants of Real Estate Returns."
Belgian capital gains tax on land
Land held >8 years is sold completely tax-free in Belgium (no meerwaardebelasting). Notary and transaction costs of ~1–2% are implicitly absorbed in the conservative appreciation assumption. Source: FOD Financiën, Wetboek van de Inkomstenbelastingen art. 90, 8°.
Pension
Belgian state pension, inflation-indexed from age 67
Belgian wettelijk pensioen is indexed to the consumer price index via the health index mechanism (spilindex). The simulation inflation-indexes the pension amount each year, which reflects actual Belgian pension law. The statutory retirement age is 67 from 2030 onward. Source: Federale Pensioendienst, Wet van 10 augustus 2015.
Stagflation + 1929 scenario
Depression-style crash + 1970s stagflation overlay
The 1929 equity path is derived from the Dow Jones Industrial Average decline of 1929–1932 (−89% peak to trough), scaled to the MSCI World context. The stagflation inflation overlay is based on US CPI during 1973–1982 (peak 14.8% in 1980) applied at a European scale. The combination represents the worst plausible scenario: equities collapse while cost of living rises, destroying both the ETF portfolio and the real value of cash simultaneously. The default path is calibrated to be severe but historically grounded — not a tail risk invented for the app. Sources: Shiller (2005) "Irrational Exuberance"; FRED US CPI data 1970–1985; MSCI World reconstructed pre-1987 data.
Failure definition
Liquid portfolio reaches zero AND land already sold
A simulation path is counted as "failed" only when both the liquid portfolio (ETF + cash) hits zero and the land has already been sold and spent. This means the land sale acts as a mandatory last-resort rescue — failure only occurs if even the land proceeds are exhausted. This is a conservative and realistic definition: it assumes land can always be sold, which may not hold in severe economic conditions. Home equity (50%) is excluded entirely as an additional unlisted buffer.
AI bubble scenario — calibration
−40% crash over 3 years, 7-year recovery, inflation stays elevated
The AI bubble scenario is modelled as a dot-com style valuation correction, but calibrated for the current macro environment rather than the 2000–2003 precedent.

Why −40% (not the 2000 Nasdaq −78%): IWDA/WEBN are diversified global indices — energy, healthcare, financials, and non-tech industrials buffer the tech collapse. In 2000–2003 MSCI World fell ~50%; with today's higher tech concentration (15–18% vs ~10% in 2000) but broader global exposure, −40% is a reasonable central estimate. Adjustable down to −70% for a Nasdaq-level scenario.

Why 7-year recovery (not 5): In 2000–2003, the Fed cut rates from 6.5% to 1% — the most aggressive easing in decades — which reflated asset prices. Today the Fed starts from ~4.5% and faces: (a) sticky inflation of 3–4% making aggressive cuts politically difficult; (b) $39T debt meaning long-term bond yields may not fall even if the Fed cuts short rates; (c) a bond market that is already pricing significant US fiscal risk. Without the same rate-cutting rocket fuel, equity recovery grinds rather than bounces.

Why inflation stays at ~3% during the crash: In 2000–2003, inflation fell to ~1.5% during the recession, giving the Fed room to cut. Today, deglobalisation, wage indexation, and energy transition costs create an inflation floor — prices are sticky downward. This limits the Fed's cutting ability and slows recovery.

Bond behaviour: Bonds rally modestly (+2% above normal) rather than strongly as in 2000 (+5–6%). The US Treasury market faces its own headwinds: record supply from deficit spending, foreign central banks diversifying away from dollars, and term premium repricing. The flight-to-safety effect is real but dampened. Sources: Fed funds rate history; MSCI World 2000–2003; CBO Debt Projections; Dalio (2021).
Fiscal trap and bond/cash assumptions
Why bonds earn modest real returns and cash loses real value
Both the bond (3.5% default return) and cash (0% default) assumptions are influenced by the US fiscal trap dynamic, which is the dominant macro force for the next decade.

The fiscal trap in brief: The US pays ~$970B/year in interest on $39T of debt. Half of all outstanding debt matures by 2026 and must be refinanced at current rates. This creates a trilemma: (1) hike rates → debt spiral accelerates, banking system stressed; (2) cut rates → bond market vigilantes demand higher yields to compensate for inflation risk, long rates rise even if short rates fall; (3) print money → inflation, but with weaker global dollar absorption now than in 2008–2020. The most likely resolution is financial repression: holding nominal rates below inflation for an extended period, slowly eroding debt in real terms.

Implications for bonds: Financial repression is structurally bad for bond holders — you receive a nominal coupon but lose real purchasing power. The 30% Belgian roerende voorheffing on bond income means you also pay tax on nominal gains that may be real losses. Belgian government bonds are partially insulated from US dynamics (ECB has its own mandate and Belgium's debt is high but manageable), but global rate dynamics set the floor.

Implications for cash: At 3.5% inflation and 0% nominal return, cash loses 3.5% real value per year. On €500k of cash over 10 years, this is ~€150k of real purchasing power destroyed — silently, without any single dramatic event. This is the primary argument for holding more ETF and less cash in a FIRE portfolio despite the volatility trade-off. Sources: US Treasury interest payment data; CBO Budget Outlook 2026; NBB Belgian government debt statistics; ECB monetary policy framework.
Monte Carlo method
Shared paths, normally-distributed shocks
Each simulation run generates N random market paths first (using Box-Muller normal distribution), then runs all allocations on the identical paths. This ensures fair comparison — the only difference between 30% and 50% ETF is the allocation, not luck of the draw. Returns and inflation are drawn independently each year (no autocorrelation modelled), which is a simplification — real markets show some momentum and mean-reversion. This makes the model slightly more volatile than reality in short windows but unbiased over long horizons.
